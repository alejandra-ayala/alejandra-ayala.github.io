The Trade Desk  ·  Culture Transformation  ·  2024

ICEE: Building a service-first identity from a team that felt invisible

When people don't have language for their own value, they protect themselves with defensiveness. My job was to give them better language — and keep it alive every single day until it became who they were.

8/10
Team members transitioned or replaced within 12 months
25
Talent operations specialists in full operational footprint
1
Shared operating standard built from zero

What I inherited

When I arrived at The Trade Desk, the Candidate Experience team had a culture of "we're not their assistant." Recruiters, sourcers, and talent operations specialists were not working as one team. There was finger-pointing, passive aggression, and a default to escalating conflict upward rather than resolving it peer-to-peer.

When I took the time to actually listen, what I found underneath the defensiveness was simpler: they felt small. They felt like their work was invisible and their contributions went unrecognized. The attitude was a protection mechanism, not a personality flaw. You can't fix a culture with a process. You have to give people a new identity to step into.

Building ICEE

I created ICEE — Inclusion, Courtesy, Experience, Efficiency — as the team's operating standard. Not a policy. Not a values statement on a wall. A living language used in every conversation, every one-on-one, every coaching moment.

I
Inclusion
Every candidate, recruiter, and stakeholder belongs in the experience we create.
C
Courtesy
How we treat people — in every interaction, at every level — is non-negotiable.
E
Experience
We are stewards of the candidate and employee experience, not administrators of it.
E
Efficiency
We deliver with precision. Speed and care are not in conflict.

The framework became the language of every conversation. "Tell me about the last time you were IC with a hiring manager." "How did you bring the E of Experience into that candidate interaction?" The standard stayed alive because leadership kept it in the room — not as a checkbox, but as a daily lens.

"Everything starts in language, and everything starts in leadership."

What the culture shift produced

Within twelve months, eight out of ten team members had been transitioned, promoted, or replaced. Two were promoted laterally into recruiter roles — a direct outcome of the development investment. Two were separated following performance management processes. Two self-selected out when the new standard made clear the old way of operating was no longer acceptable. Two stayed and thrived.

The churn was not failure. The churn was the culture working. When you raise the standard, the people who can't meet it leave — and the ones who remain are the ones who belong.

The Trade Desk  ·  Communication Culture  ·  2024

From noise to collaboration: eliminating a cross-functional escalation culture

A team that had normalized passive aggression and upward tattling. The fix wasn't a policy. It was a framework — and the discipline to hold it until new behavior became the default.

High
Volume of leadership escalations at start
Near zero
Cross-functional escalations after implementation

The situation

Recruiters, sourcers, and talent operations specialists were not resolving conflict with each other — they were bringing it to leadership as a default. A culture of telling on each other, passive aggressive dynamics, and us-versus-them tension running in every direction. Leadership bandwidth was being consumed by noise the team should have been equipped to handle themselves.

I also recognized a communication style mismatch early. I operate directly and assertively. The tenured team members — some with three to eight years at the company — communicated in a more feelings-based, indirect style. Before I could change anything, I needed to understand the room. I secured an internal senior HR mentor who recommended Crucial Conversations, and I read it with the specific problem in mind.

The escalation framework

From the book I extracted a five-step model for how conflict and escalation should work — and made it a non-negotiable standard across the entire function.

1Incident. Something happened once. Note it. Don't escalate.
2Coincidence. It happened twice. Pay attention. Still don't escalate.
3Pattern. Three times. Now you have something real to address.
4Direct conversation. Address it with the person directly — before anyone else is involved.
5Escalation. Only now, having completed steps one through four, do you bring leadership in.

Recruiters had to complete every step before bringing me an escalation about a talent operations specialist. The sourcing manager and recruiting directors applied the same standard. Not optional. Not situational.

"We wanted to create a culture of: we are all one team, and we will deal with each other directly."

What changed

The noise stopped. People started developing their own working relationships without leadership in the middle. Recruiters and specialists who had previously been in constant conflict started collaborating effectively because they had a structure for handling friction when it arose. The escalation framework didn't just reduce noise — it forced the team to develop interpersonal skills they'd never been required to use. That is leadership development embedded in an operational standard.

Pathfinders: A six-session career development program built post-layoff with zero budget

After a workforce reduction left the team scared and demoralized, I built something to move toward. Not a morale initiative. A career development program with real curriculum, real facilitators, and a live finale that felt like a radio show.

The situation

GitLab conducted a 7% workforce reduction in 2022. Sourcers were eliminated entirely, half the recruiters were gone, and the people who remained were scared and grieving. Recruiting was paused. The team had bandwidth for the first time in years — and nothing to channel it into. I had no budget. I had a demoralized team. And I had a clear belief: learning and growth are retention tools even when money isn't available.

What I built

I designed Pathfinders as a six-session internal career development series for levels four through six across the entire HR and People division — including contractors. Each session was facilitated by a director or VP from within the department, creating visibility for senior leaders while developing the people below them. The curriculum was designed to meet people where they were emotionally and give them something concrete to move toward:

  • Critical thinking and the five whys
  • Communicating with VP and C-suite stakeholders as a coordinator or specialist
  • Leading without authority
  • Pivoting into your next career move and navigating internal mobility
  • A fishbowl interview featuring TA professionals who had started as specialists — showing the audience their own possible futures
  • A live interview with the Head of TA, open to the entire department as a drop-in
"The last session felt like a radio show. People recognized my interviewing instincts out loud, in front of everyone."

The results

Pathfinders produced two promotions and one internal role transition directly attributable to skills and visibility built through the program. It served the entire HR division across 60+ countries at a moment of organizational crisis — not just my direct team. The fishbowl finale was also a personal revelation. Facilitating that live interview — holding the room, drawing out real stories, making people feel seen — was the moment I understood that this work is where I am most fully myself professionally.

GitLab & The Trade Desk  ·  Hiring Philosophy  ·  2021 – Present

Hiring for attitude over aptitude: a methodology with zero bad hires across a decade

Skills can be taught. Drive, resourcefulness, and character cannot. I built a repeatable interview system around that belief — and it has never failed.

0
Bad hires using this methodology across a full career
15+
Hires made across GitLab and The Trade Desk
100s
Hiring managers trained through Hire Well at TTD

The philosophy

I hire for attitude over aptitude. I can teach a system, a tool, a process. I cannot teach someone to be resourceful, to show initiative when no one is watching, or to lead before they have the title. Those qualities either exist or they don't — and most interview panels are not designed to find them. My methodology is built around one question that runs underneath every other question in the interview: does this person create their own opportunities, or do they wait for them?

How it works

I design the entire interview panel intentionally. I select each person based on what they are uniquely positioned to assess, and I prep the full panel before a single candidate comes in on exactly what to listen for. The debrief is structured, not a conversation. Every interviewer enters it with a framework.

For career pivoters and less experienced candidates, I built a specific assessment lens — evaluating transferable skills, systems thinking, prioritization instinct, and the ability to scale and teach what they build — rather than role-specific jargon or prior experience in the exact function.

"I hired one person because while waiting a year for her work permit, she created an HR volunteer role at a local nonprofit from scratch. If she'll do that for free, what will she do for a great job and a great manager?"

Hire Well at The Trade Desk

At The Trade Desk, I was selected to facilitate Hire Well — a company-wide interviewer training program — to hiring managers and VPs across the entire organization, both in-person and globally online. I taught panel design, scorecard writing, bias elimination, and structured debrief methodology. Facilitation is a distinct skill from curriculum design — and being trusted to stand in front of VPs and change the way they hire is a different kind of validation.

What the methodology produces

At GitLab, I hired globally across 60+ countries for a team that grew to 15. Several of those hires were people others would have passed over — career pivoters, candidates without pedigree but with unmistakable drive. Multiple were promoted. One became the team lead after I left. I still mentor one of them today.

GitLab  ·  TA Operations  ·  2022

From 30% to 4%: A 26-point gain in offer acceptance — in six months

A 30% offer decline rate is not a compensation problem. It is a signal that something is breaking between candidate expectations and what they experience in the process. I found what was breaking and rebuilt it.

30%
Offer decline rate at start
4%
Offer decline rate at six months
+26pt
Improvement in offer acceptance rate

The situation

When I inherited the Global Candidate Experience function at GitLab, one metric stood out immediately: 30% of candidates who received offers were declining them. Across a globally distributed team operating in 60+ countries, that represented an enormous investment in sourcing, interviewing, and coordination walking out the door at the finish line. The instinct in recruiting is to call this a compensation problem. I didn't assume that. I started with data.

Root cause analysis

I pulled decline reasons from exit surveys, recruiter debrief notes, and direct candidate feedback. What emerged was not a compensation story — it was a communication and expectation story:

  • Candidates experienced the interview process as disconnected — each stage felt like it came from a different company
  • Recruiters were not consistently checking candidate intent before extending offers, leaving motivation gaps unaddressed
  • Time between final interview and offer was long enough for competing offers to land and close first
  • Hiring managers were not aligned on how to speak about compensation, growth, and culture during the process
"The fix was operational and behavioral, not budgetary."

What I built

I redesigned the recruiter touchpoint cadence to include explicit intent-checking conversations at key stages. I built and delivered enablement sessions for recruiters on how to surface and address candidate hesitation before the offer stage. I worked with hiring managers to create consistent messaging frameworks around growth, culture, and compensation. I also consolidated the global TA operations playbook — giving recruiters a consistent, reliable process that became the primary enablement resource across the GitLab Handbook.

The result

Within six months, offer decline rate dropped from 30% to 4% — a 26-point improvement across 60+ countries. Every declined offer represents re-sourcing, re-interviewing, and re-coordinating at significant cost. But the more important outcome is what this demonstrated: recruiting operations, done well, is not administrative support. It is a strategic lever on business outcomes.

The Trade Desk  ·  Process Design  ·  2024

65-question time-in-motion study: turning analysis into 25 improvement projects

Most ops leaders identify bottlenecks. I mapped every single moment of friction across the full candidate journey — then turned the findings into professional development opportunities for my team instead of just a project list for me.

65
Questions in the time-in-motion study
25%
Reduction in coordination workload
35%
Increase in team productivity

The approach

When I arrived at The Trade Desk, I was overseeing a function with 25 talent operations specialists across North America and global regions. Before I could improve anything, I needed to understand everything. I designed a 65-question time-in-motion study that mapped the full candidate journey end-to-end: every touchpoint, every handoff, every place where coordination was happening manually that didn't need to be, and every place where the process was creating friction for candidates, recruiters, or hiring teams.

What made this different

Most leaders who run a process audit produce a project list and assign it to themselves or a small workgroup. I translated the 25 findings into 25 discrete improvement projects — and delegated each one as a professional development opportunity for a team member. The analysis became the curriculum. This approach did two things simultaneously: it drove the operational outcomes the business needed, and it gave the team real, scoped ownership over meaningful work.

"Every bottleneck became someone's development opportunity."

The results

The 25 improvement projects collectively reduced coordination workload by 25% and increased overall team productivity by up to 35%. Those numbers came from a team already under significant pressure from the broader organizational context I had inherited — which makes the improvement more significant, not less. The time-in-motion methodology is now a tool I carry into every operations role. Understanding where time actually goes — not where people think it goes — is the prerequisite for building anything that works at scale.
